# Replacing the Heuristic Route Matcher with a Principled Bayesian Estimator — Research Report

Date: 2026-06-10 · Produced by deep-research agent (web sources cited inline)

## 1. The core finding: your problem is a solved class of problem

Route-constrained localization with noisy, intermittently-informative observations is exactly the problem the **Hidden Markov map-matching** literature solved for vehicles. Newson & Krumm's 2009 SIGSPATIAL paper ([PDF](https://www.ismll.uni-hildesheim.de/lehre/semSpatial-10s/script/6.pdf)) is the canonical design: hidden states are positions on the road network, observations are noisy measurements, an **emission probability** scores how well a measurement fits a candidate position, and a **transition probability** scores whether the implied movement between consecutive measurements is physically plausible. The decisive insight: they replaced exactly the kind of threshold-gate logic we have now ("correct matches tend to be nearby, successive matches tend to be linked by simple routes, some points are junk and should be ignored") with two probability densities and a Viterbi/forward pass — and their parameters were **estimated from data, not hand-tuned**: emission noise σ_z = 1.4826 × median absolute deviation of measurement-to-matched-position distance (4.07 m on their data), and transition scale β = median(|route distance − straight-line distance|)/ln 2, using a robust exponential-scale estimator. This is the template for killing the six-condition gate: every "threshold" becomes a noise parameter estimated by replaying surveys.

Production map-matchers are direct descendants. **Valhalla's Meili** ([algorithm docs](https://valhalla.github.io/valhalla/meili/algorithms/), [implementation details](https://valhalla.github.io/valhalla/meili/implementation_details/)) uses a Gaussian emission `c·exp(−d²/2σ²)`, an exponential transition on |route_dist − measured_dist|, converts to costs via `−log10(p)`, and runs Viterbi as a Dijkstra-style shortest path. OSRM's matcher is the same HMM design ([Telenav's annotated spec](https://github.com/Telenav/open-source-spec/blob/master/osrm/doc/osrm_mapmatching.md)). For **online** (real-time, not post-hoc) operation, Goh et al. extended the HMM with a variable sliding window that emits the matched state once the optimal prefix is provably stable ([MIT paper, ITSC 2012](https://www.mit.edu/~jaillet/general/map_matching_itsc2012-final.pdf)) — i.e., a fixed-lag forward pass, which is what you want on-device rather than full-trace Viterbi.

Our state space is even friendlier: it's **one-dimensional** (arc position along a known route, measured in survey-profile index units or steps). That means no particle filter needed — a **discrete-grid Bayes filter (HMM over discretized route distance)** is exact, deterministic, and cheap. The magnetic indoor-positioning literature confirms HMM/grid filters over discretized position with PDR-driven transitions and magnetic emissions are a standard, effective design ([survey of magnetic-field-based indoor localization, Electronics 2022](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-9292/11/6/864); [HMM+PDR pedestrian positioning](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5038725/)).

## 2. Observation models: turning signals into likelihoods

**Magnetic profile likelihood (pointwise Gaussian).** Survey profiles already store per-point `mean[]` and `stddev[]` (`survey-recorder/SurveyRecorder/RouteProfile.swift:82-85`) — that *is* a Gaussian observation model. For a hypothesized route position s, the likelihood of a ~2 s magnitude window m₁..m_k is ∏ᵢ N(mᵢ; mean(s+i), stddev(s+i)² + σ_sensor²), evaluated in log space. This is precisely how magnetic particle-filter systems weight hypotheses: geomagnetic PF weighting uses a single-variable Gaussian density on the difference between observed and mapped field intensity ([Huang et al., Journal of Sensors 2018](https://www.hindawi.com/journals/js/2018/5989678/); [Ning & Chen, Sensors 2020](https://www.mdpi.com/1424-8220/20/1/185), who got 0.6–0.8 m fusing PF magnetic matching with PDR on a smartphone). Two practical refinements from **Magicol** (Shu et al., IEEE JSAC 2015, [PDF](https://yshu.org/paper/jsac15magicol.pdf)), the closest published system to ours: (a) **mean-remove both the live window and the profile window before comparing**, to cancel device hard-iron bias and per-session offsets; (b) score the window with a distance d (they use DTW to absorb speed variation) and convert to a weight κ = exp(−d²/2σ²), where σ "reflects the overall disturbance intensity of the indoor magnetic field" — one parameter, fitted from data, instead of the correlation/confidence/match-position triple gate. Magicol's motion model is also instructive: step length perturbed by Gaussian noise with σ = 0.2·step_length, heading noise 10°, and map-violating particles down-weighted ×0.01 rather than killed.

**If keeping Pearson r as the feature:** a correlation coefficient can be made into a proper likelihood via the **Fisher z-transform**: z = atanh(r) is approximately normal with variance 1/(n−3) where n is the number of (effectively independent) samples in the window ([Statology](https://www.statology.org/fisher-z-transformation/); [SAS/IML explanation](https://blogs.sas.com/content/iml/2017/09/20/fishers-transformation-correlation.html)). Model p(z | on-route at s) ≈ N(z; μ_match, 1/(n−3)+τ²) and p(z | elsewhere) ≈ N(z; μ_null, …), with μ_match and μ_null measured empirically from replayed surveys (distribution of r at the true position vs. at random positions). This gives a likelihood *ratio* per window instead of a hard 0.68 cutoff. However, the pointwise Gaussian on raw (mean-removed) magnitude is preferable: it uses the stddev arrays, handles low-variance segments gracefully (where r is meaningless but the absolute level still constrains position), and avoids the full-segment argmax search — the filter evaluates the likelihood *at each hypothesis* rather than asking "where does this window best match?", which is what makes multi-modal posteriors and backtracking work.

**Steps/time and motion class** belong in the **transition model**, not the observation model: detected steps drive how far probability mass advances; the walking/standing classification selects between an "advance by ~1 step ± noise" kernel and a "stay put" kernel. Per-segment median steps/duration calibrate the mapping from steps to profile-index units.

## 3. Off-route detection as model selection, not a counter

Three principled mechanisms, in increasing order of sophistication:

**(a) Null/junk handling by likelihood floor (Newson-Krumm style).** Newson & Krumm zero out emission beyond 200 m and detect "HMM breaks" — time steps where all transitions go to zero — then excise the offending observations and, if a break persists >180 s, declare the trace unmatchable ([paper](https://www.ismll.uni-hildesheim.de/lehre/semSpatial-10s/script/6.pdf), §4.2). The online analog: floor every emission at a small ε so the filter never divides by zero, and monitor how often the floor binds.

**(b) Explicit off-route state — the recommended approach.** Lyft's map-matching team formalized "the map can't explain the motion" as **model selection between an on-road HMM and a free-space (off-road) Kalman filter run in parallel** — a "semi-interacting multiple model" (sIMM) filter that computes a posterior **model probability** at each step from model-conditioned observation likelihoods, switching to off-road when the road network "cannot plausibly accommodate the observed vehicle motion," and using sustained off-road probability to flag map errors ([Murphy, Pao & Yuen, arXiv:1809.09755](https://arxiv.org/abs/1809.09755)). For the 1D case this collapses to something trivial to implement: add **one extra discrete state "OFF"** with a constant emission density u (the expected likelihood of a magnetic window against an *unrelated* profile — measure it by cross-matching segments in replay), transition probability λ of entering it from any route position, and exits back to route positions near the last on-route mode. Off-route is then simply **P(OFF | data) > threshold for T seconds** — one interpretable number replacing the high-PDR-without-agreement counter of 12. This is also how a likelihood-ratio test naturally appears: log P(window|route)/P(window|OFF) accumulates over time, which is **Wald's sequential probability ratio test (SPRT)** — optimal in requiring the fewest samples to decide between two hypotheses at given type-I/type-II error rates ([SPRT overview, ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/engineering/sequential-probability-ratio-test); change-detection variant: [Shiryaev SPRT](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/780416/)).

**(c) Likelihood-degradation monitoring (Augmented MCL).** Robotics' standard "am I lost?" signal: track short-term (w_fast) and long-term (w_slow) exponential averages of the mean observation likelihood; when w_fast/w_slow collapses, the filter is diverging — AMCL injects max{0, 1 − w_fast/w_slow} random particles to relocalize ([Thrun, Fox, Burgard & Dellaert, "Robust Monte Carlo Localization for Mobile Robots"](http://robots.stanford.edu/papers/thrun.robust-mcl.pdf); [Probabilistic Robotics notes](https://calvinfeng.gitbook.io/probabilistic-robotics/localization/grid-and-monte-carlo/monte-carlo-localization)). In a grid filter the equivalent of effective-sample-size collapse is the **normalization constant** of each Bayes update (the marginal likelihood of the observation): a sustained drop below its replay-calibrated baseline is the "kidnapped" signal ([kidnapped-robot detection survey/DTW approach](https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/11/4/1580/htm)). Production navigation SDKs expose exactly this as a boolean off-route event driving rerouting ([Mapbox Navigation rerouting docs](https://docs.mapbox.com/android/navigation/guides/turn-by-turn-navigation/rerouting-and-refresh/)) — thresholds are internal, but the architecture (continuous matcher + off-route monitor + reroute trigger) is the standard. Use (b) as the primary mechanism and (c) as a cheap sanity diagnostic.

## 4. Checkpoint advance as a decision rule

With a posterior P(s | data) over route position, "have we reached checkpoint c?" becomes computing **P(s ≥ s_c | data)** and applying Bayesian decision theory: advance when posterior probability exceeds τ = C_FA/(C_FA + C_miss), where C_FA is the cost of a false advance and C_miss the cost of a delayed detection — making the false-advance/missed-detection tradeoff an explicit, tunable cost ratio instead of six entangled gates. For the time-extended version (sustained evidence before committing), that is again **SPRT**: accumulate log-likelihood ratio of "past checkpoint" vs "before checkpoint" and decide at boundaries set by chosen error rates α, β ([SPRT](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/engineering/sequential-probability-ratio-test)). Note Magicol's pragmatic reporting hybrid — max-weight estimate early (locks on fast), switch to weighted mean of the top-50% mass once converged (stable) ([Magicol](https://yshu.org/paper/jsac15magicol.pdf)) — but for *decisions*, integrate the posterior tail; never ratchet the point estimate. The ratchet is the root cause of the current fragility: it discards the posterior's ability to retreat when evidence reverses.

## 5. Multi-hypothesis: backtracking, pauses, wrong direction

A full posterior (grid or particles) **is** the multi-hypothesis tracker — competing position hypotheses coexist until evidence resolves them, which is exactly why HMM approaches displaced geometric matchers ("concurrently evaluate multiple hypotheses of the actual mapping," [Goh et al.](https://www.mit.edu/~jaillet/general/map_matching_itsc2012-final.pdf)). Concretely: give the step-transition kernel a small backward component (e.g., 90% forward / 8% stay / 2% backward per detected step) so backtracking is representable; pauses are handled by the standing-mode identity kernel; wrong-direction walking on the right route shows up as posterior mass moving backward — the UI can surface "moving away from checkpoint" instead of silently stalling. Keeping *memorized multiple candidates* rather than committing early is also explicitly shown to fix HMM map-matching failures at ambiguous junctions ([Wang et al., robust map matching with memorized candidates](https://yongcaiwang.github.io/papers/mapmatching.pdf)). One caveat from Magicol: magnetic magnitude is weakly discriminative pointwise — distant locations can look alike while neighbors differ ([Magicol Fig. 9 discussion](https://yshu.org/paper/jsac15magicol.pdf)) — so expect multi-modal posteriors early in a segment and let the step-driven transition disambiguate; don't force unimodality.

## 6. Offline tuning and evaluation

- **Parameter fitting from replays, not grid search:** Newson & Krumm's recipe — fit σ from the MAD of (observation − ground-truth position) residuals and the transition scale from the median motion discrepancy ([paper](https://www.ismll.uni-hildesheim.de/lehre/semSpatial-10s/script/6.pdf), §5.2). For us: replay each recorded session against its own profile with hand-labeled checkpoint times, collect magnetic residuals at true positions → σ_sensor; collect step-to-profile-index ratios → transition noise; cross-match wrong segments → the OFF-state emission constant u.
- **Metrics:** Newson & Krumm's *route mismatch fraction* (incorrect route length added+subtracted ÷ true length) for trajectory-level accuracy; the indoor-positioning community's standard is the **third quartile (75th percentile) of positioning error**, chosen for outlier robustness in the EvAAL/IPIN competitions, which also pioneered exactly the offline replay-on-recorded-trials evaluation we need ([EvAAL framework, Sensors 2017](https://www.mdpi.com/1424-8220/17/10/2327); [IPIN 2020 offline evaluation, IEEE](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/abstract/document/9439493)). For the checkpoint use-case add: checkpoint detection rate within ±k steps of labeled truth, false-advance rate per session, median time-to-detection after true arrival, and off-route detection delay / false-alarm rate on deliberately-off-route recordings.

## 7. Recommended architecture for the prototype

**State:** discrete grid over route arc-position s in profile-index units (~1 sample ≈ 1 PDR step; a full route of a few hundred meters → 500–2,000 bins), plus one OFF state. One `[Double]` log-belief array; updates are O(bins × kernel width) per step event — microseconds in Swift, no resampling, fully deterministic and unit-testable. (A particle filter buys nothing in 1D; grid filters are the exact version of the same Bayes filter ([Probabilistic Robotics](https://calvinfeng.gitbook.io/probabilistic-robotics/localization/grid-and-monte-carlo/monte-carlo-localization)).)

**Transition model (per detected step / per second):** walking → convolve belief with an asymmetric kernel (mean +1 index, σ from replay-fitted step noise, ~0.2·step à la [Magicol](https://yshu.org/paper/jsac15magicol.pdf), small backward tail); standing → identity (tiny diffusion); transition segments (`useForMatching == false`) get duration-median-driven advance with inflated σ. Any state → OFF with probability λ; OFF → re-entry kernel centered on last on-route mode.

**Observation model (per ~0.5 s):** mean-removed pointwise Gaussian log-likelihood of the recent magnitude window against `magneticMagnitude.mean/stddev` at each hypothesis (variance = stddevᵢ² + σ_sensor²); OFF state emits constant log u. Optionally retain the Pearson-r pipeline initially via Fisher-z likelihoods (variance 1/(n−3), [ref](https://blogs.sas.com/content/iml/2017/09/20/fishers-transformation-correlation.html)) to de-risk migration.

**Off-route:** report when P(OFF) > 0.5 sustained for T≈5 s (sIMM-style model probability, [Lyft](https://arxiv.org/abs/1809.09755)); log w_fast/w_slow of the per-update marginal likelihood as a diagnostic ([AMCL](http://robots.stanford.edu/papers/thrun.robust-mcl.pdf)).

**Checkpoint advance:** trigger when P(s ≥ s_checkpoint) > τ with τ = C_FA/(C_FA+C_miss) (start τ≈0.8), optionally requiring two consecutive updates (poor-man's SPRT). No ratchet; if mass retreats, the UI estimate may retreat with hysteresis.

**Migration path:** (1) Build a replay harness running recorded sessions through both estimators (recordings exist — Meadows sessions in-repo) and label checkpoint times. (2) Implement the observation likelihood and validate it alone: plot log-likelihood heatmaps (position × time) per session; the true trajectory should be a visible ridge. (3) Add the transition model + forward pass; tune σ_sensor, step noise, λ, u analytically from replays (Newson-Krumm recipe). (4) Run in shadow mode inside `LivePositioningController`, logging both estimators' decisions. (5) Compare on the §6 metrics; flip the UI to the filter; delete the gates. The schema needs nothing new — `RouteProfile`'s mean/stddev arrays and step/duration medians are already a complete generative model.
