## 1. Features: what the strong systems actually feed the matcher

**Magnitude-only is the historical default, but the modern consensus is a 2D rotation-invariant pair: magnitude + gravity-projected vertical component.** Magicol (Shu et al., IEEE JSAC 2015) explicitly argued that using the raw 3D vector requires either continuous attitude tracking (defeated by gyro drift) or a fixed phone pose, so "only the magnitude of the magnetic signal may be used in practice" ([Magicol PDF](https://yshu.org/paper/jsac15magicol.pdf), [IEEE](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/7102692/)). MaLoc (Xie et al., UbiComp 2014) went further: it extracts the **horizontal and vertical components (Bh, Bv) in the gravity frame** — both rotation-invariant because gravity is observable from the accelerometer — and showed the "HV fingerprint" is markedly more unique than magnitude alone. MaLoc uses a *hybrid* model: HV fingerprints to converge fast, then magnitude for steady-state tracking, because HV is noisier while walking (gravity estimate degrades under dynamics) ([MaLoc PDF](https://taogu.site/pub/paper/MaLoc-%20A%20Practical%20Magnetic%20Fingerprinting%20Approach%20to%20Indoor%20Localization%20using%20Smartphones.pdf), [ACM](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/2632048.2632057)).

A 2026 arXiv study (MagNet, evaluated on the MagPIE dataset) directly compared raw 3D input vs the invariant pair (Mn = norm, Mg = projection onto gravity): the 3D vector degrades catastrophically under device rotation (~14 m error at 90° rotation), while the 2D invariant features hold constant accuracy and *beat* 3D inputs once rotation exceeds 0–6° — i.e., essentially always in handheld use ([arXiv 2604.22896](https://arxiv.org/html/2604.22896)). The Sensors 2022 measurement study likewise found transformed horizontal/vertical components (mh, mv) outperform raw axes for orientation-independent positioning ([PMC9183029](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9183029/)).

**Gradients/differentials are the standard device-invariance trick.** MaLoc observed that two Galaxy Nexus phones read different absolute values along the same corridor but "the gradients of these two curves remain quite constant," so they match on step-to-step differences to avoid per-device calibration. FollowMe (Shu et al., MobiCom 2015 — the closest system to our use case: following a previously recorded route trace) low-pass filters the magnitude and then matches on the **differential (first-difference) signal**, explicitly because online mean-removal fails early in a walk when you haven't seen enough of the trace to know its mean ([FollowMe PDF](https://yshu.org/paper/mobicom15followme.pdf)). MaLoc also found vertical sensor height (0.5/1/1.5 m) changes absolute values but leaves gradients similar — useful given users hold phones at different heights.

**Multi-scale features** appear in the learned-matching literature: MAIL uses multi-scale attention over geomagnetic sequences ([ACM IMWUT 2020](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3397335)), and multi-scale TCN+LSTM models concatenate features at several temporal receptive fields ([arXiv 2109.11750](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2109.11750)); MagNet's dilated CNN (dilations 1→64 over a 4 s window at 50 Hz) is effectively a learned multi-scale filter bank ([arXiv 2604.22896](https://arxiv.org/html/2604.22896)).

## 2. Matching: what beats a naive Pearson window

**DTW with a step-derived band, on differenced signals, is the workhorse.** FollowMe's step-constrained online DTW is almost a drop-in design for our problem: it maintains a banded warping-cost matrix whose band position/width is driven by step detection (linearithmic time, bounded memory), adaptively widens the search band when similarity is low, and bounds the allowed divergence between the follower's position estimate and reference index. Result: **95% of spatial errors within 2 m** while following a route in a four-story building, phone-pose-free ([FollowMe PDF](https://yshu.org/paper/mobicom15followme.pdf), [ACM](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/2789168.2790099)). Magicol's variant: vectorize samples **per detected step** (so compared vectors cover similar spatial distance), mean-remove both vectors, then DTW; the DTW distance d feeds a Gaussian particle weight exp(−d²/2σ²) where **σ is tuned to the building's overall magnetic disturbance intensity** — a crude but principled observation-noise model ([Magicol PDF](https://yshu.org/paper/jsac15magicol.pdf)).

Key practical numbers: Magicol needs **~20 s of walking** before magnitude sequences become discriminative enough to localize; below that, ambiguity dominates. MagNet uses 4 s windows (200 samples @ 50 Hz) but with learned features. A 2 s window is well below what the literature considers reliable for raw correlation — this is the root cause of spurious matches. Per-step spatial resampling (samples-per-step vectors) rather than time resampling is what both Magicol and FollowMe use to neutralize walking-speed variation, which Pearson-on-time-windows handles poorly.

**Normalization practice:** mean removal (Magicol) or differencing (MaLoc, FollowMe) is universal; full z-normalization is riskier on flat magnetic stretches because dividing by near-zero variance amplifies noise into spurious structure — the time-series literature notes improper normalization windows "miss parts of the hidden pattern or include unwanted noise" ([arXiv 1912.11977](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1912.11977)). Pearson correlation is itself z-normalized similarity, which is exactly why smooth windows correlate spuriously: after normalization, two featureless segments look identical. Distance should therefore be gated by raw signal variance/energy, not only by correlation.

**Using survey stddev:** the standard formulation is a per-point Gaussian likelihood P(z|s) with variance from the map (MaLoc Eq. 8 cites this as the common "Gaussian pseudo-distribution" model). The principled extension is Gaussian-process magnetic maps (Solin, Kok et al.), which jointly model field components via a scalar-potential GP and give a predictive mean *and* variance everywhere — used for both weighting observations and knowing where the map is uncertain ([arXiv 1509.04634](https://arxiv.org/abs/1509.04634), [GP magnetic SLAM, arXiv 1804.01926](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/1804.01926)). The unused stddev channel is exactly this: a heteroscedastic noise model, where Mahalanobis-style distance Σ((x−μ)²/σ²) replaces Pearson.

**Learned matching** (DeepML's LSTM on magnetic+light, [IEEE](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/8422562/); AMID's deep classification of magnetic landmarks, [PMC5982601](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5982601/); MAIL; MagNet) outperforms hand-crafted matching in papers, but requires training data volumes inconsistent with 3–5 survey passes. Not recommended at this stage.

## 3. Spurious matches, aliasing, and knowing where matching is possible

The non-uniqueness of geomagnetic fingerprints is acknowledged as the core failure mode, especially at large scale ([MDPI Sensors 22(23):9032](https://www.mdpi.com/1424-8220/22/23/9032)). Documented mitigations:

- **Longer sequences**: Magicol's whole thesis — a single magnitude value matches dozens of locations on a 50 m corridor; a 3-sample sequence matches two; per-step vectors over ~20 s are near-unique. Sequence/sliding-window fingerprints are explicitly framed as a distinguishability-increasing mechanism ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-030-95384-3_5)).
- **Explicit failure detection**: MaLoc's localization-quality estimator uses the *clustering degree* of particle hypotheses (multi-modal/dispersed = ambiguous) and detects **92.6% of localization failures** (kidnapped-robot events), triggering a recovery procedure. The route-constrained analogue: track the top-k correlation peaks along the segment; if the second-best peak is close to the best, declare ambiguity and coast on PDR.
- **Fingerprint accuracy indicators**: recent geomagnetic/PDR fusion work computes a combined fingerprint accuracy indication to adaptively weight magnetic vs PDR ([IEEE 10303280](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/10303280/)); confidence-threshold filtering of candidate matches is standard ([PMC9739983](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9739983/)).
- **Distinctiveness from the map itself**: areas near pillars/steel show distinctive anomalies usable as landmarks, while "normal ground" has many same-valued points ([PMC8434502](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8434502/)). Since we own the survey, per-position distinctiveness can be precomputed offline (e.g., autocorrelation of the segment profile, or min distance from each window to all other windows of the same segment) — the offline analogue of MaLoc's online quality score; directly answers "which parts of the route are matchable."

No published "null-distribution calibration" for magnetic matching specifically was found; the closest practices are Magicol's building-level σ and conformal/Bayesian-adaptive approaches ([arXiv 2505.08639](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2505.08639)). Calibrating a per-segment null (correlation of live windows against *wrong* offsets, from our own survey passes) is cheap and would be ahead of common practice.

## 4. Reported real-world accuracy and IndoorAtlas

- **Magicol** (magnetic-only, 90th percentile): **5 m office corridors, 1 m underground garage, 8 m supermarket** — open, low-steel retail space is the worst case; structured corridors are good; garages (dense rebar, cars) are best ([Magicol](https://yshu.org/paper/jsac15magicol.pdf)).
- **MaLoc**: 1–2.8 m mean in a 72×64 m building, multiple phones, no pose restriction ([ACM](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/2632048.2632057)).
- **FollowMe** (route-following): 95% within 2 m ([MobiCom](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/2789168.2790099)).
- Recent magnetometer-PDR fusion: **0.65 m corridor vs 0.93 m open area vs 1.38 m complex area** — corridors consistently beat open spaces, and locating users in expansive areas is flagged as the persistent hard case ([PMC12551869](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12551869/), [PMC9921884](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9921884/)).

**IndoorAtlas** (founded 2012 by Janne Haverinen, Oulu) commercialized exactly this stack: magnetic anomaly maps from steel structures + "patented multi-layer sensor fusion," claiming ~1 m accuracy with no installed hardware ([GPS World](https://www.gpsworld.com/indooratlas-announces-geomagnetic-indoor-positioning-service/), [indooratlas.com](https://www.indooratlas.com/), [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_positioning)). Their patent family is instructive: map generation from walked paths with interpolation ([US9316501](https://patents.google.com/patent/US9316501B2), [US20130177208](https://patents.google.com/patent/US20130177208)), location discovery by matching observed field sequences to the map ([US9151621](https://patents.google.com/patent/US9151621B2/en), [US8798924](https://patents.google.com/patent/US8798924)), deriving device orientation indoors from the mapped 3D field ([US20140357305](https://patents.google.com/patent/US20140357305)), and magnetic SLAM for survey-free mapping ([US9476717](https://patents.google.com/patent/US9476717)). Acknowledged weaknesses: moving metal (lifts, carts) and temporal field drift ([ResearchGate discussion](https://www.researchgate.net/post/What_are_the_weaknesses_of_the_geomagnetic_positioning_systems_such_as_IndoorAtlas)). Notably, IndoorAtlas never shipped magnetic-only: production accuracy comes from fusing magnetic + PDR + WiFi/BLE + barometer + map constraints.

## 5. Temporal stability and device variance

**Maps stay valid for months absent structural change.** A 5-month longitudinal study (Feb–Jun 2020) found magnetic trajectories overlapped across the whole period; indoor MF measurements are Gaussian with strong temporal stability ([PMC9183029](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9183029/)). MaLoc and predecessors report furniture rearrangement, carried objects, and other pedestrians have very limited effect; what breaks maps is structural/large-metal change (renovations, racks, parked vehicles — Magicol measured garage profiles differing between empty-at-midnight and full-at-4PM) and proximity to elevators/moving machinery ([MDPI survey, Electronics 11(6):864](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-9292/11/6/864), [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_positioning)).

**Device-to-device variance is mostly a constant offset + scale, fixable by calibration or design-out.** Measured same-spot means: iPhone Xs Max ~46.9–47.6 µT, Huawei P9 ~49.0–50.0 µT, industrial Bluebird ~122–125 µT (far outside the 23–62 µT geomagnetic range); cross-device matching accuracy fell from 93.3% to 59.3% without correction, and batch hard/soft-iron calibration was found "stable over time and space" (device-specific, not location-specific) ([PMC9183029](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9183029/)). This is why MaLoc/FollowMe match on gradients/differences instead.

**iPhone specifics:** `CMDeviceMotion.magneticField` returns a `CMCalibratedMagneticField` — total local field with *device* bias (hard-iron) removed, plus an `accuracy` enum (`uncalibrated/low/medium/high`) that should be read and logged; when accuracy is `.uncalibrated` the values are not trustworthy ([Apple docs](https://developer.apple.com/documentation/coremotion/cmcalibratedmagneticfield), [CMMagneticFieldCalibrationAccuracy](https://developer.apple.com/documentation/coremotion/cmmagneticfieldcalibrationaccuracy)). Two iPhone-specific hazards: (1) Apple's background calibration can re-estimate bias mid-walk, producing a step change in reported field — gradient/differenced features are immune to this, absolute profiles are not; (2) MagSafe: the iPhone's own annular magnet array, plus magnetic cases/wallets, produce stray fields exceeding 1 mT (some >50 G in contact) near the device ([PMC9919430](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC9919430/), [JAHA MagSafe study](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8477860/)). The internal array is a constant bias Apple calibrates out, but attaching/detaching a MagSafe wallet mid-route changes the hard-iron environment and forces recalibration — expect transient garbage and gate on the accuracy flag.

## Apply to our prototype (prioritized)

1. **Switch matching to differenced (gradient) signal, gated by raw-window variance.** Low-pass the magnitude, take first differences before correlating/DTW (FollowMe's exact recipe). Kills device offset, iOS recalibration jumps, and MagSafe-attach bias in one move; variance gating stops Pearson from hallucinating on flat stretches. *Impact: high (directly targets spurious-match and device-variance problems). Cost: low — a preprocessing change plus a `if rawStd < threshold: skip magnetic update` gate.*

2. **Add the vertical component Bv (projection of calibrated field onto gravity from CMDeviceMotion) as a second channel.** Profile and match (|B|, Bv) jointly; both are pose-invariant, and MaLoc/MagNet show 2D invariant features substantially out-discriminate magnitude alone. Gravity is already available from CMDeviceMotion. *Impact: high (roughly doubles fingerprint information, halves aliasing). Cost: medium — survey format change, re-survey or recompute if raw vectors were logged.*

3. **Lengthen the effective match window to 4–8 s (or N steps) and resample per-step, not per-time.** Magicol needs ~20 s to disambiguate from scratch; route-constrained, 4–8 s is workable, but 2 s is below the literature floor. Vectorize per detected step (samples-per-step) so walking-speed variation stops smearing correlations; this also makes a banded DTW natural. *Impact: high. Cost: low-medium.*

4. **Replace global Pearson slide with step-constrained banded DTW around the PDR prior.** FollowMe's algorithm (band positioned by step count, width adapted by match quality, bounded drift between estimate and reference index) is purpose-built for this scenario and ran in real time on 2015 phones, achieving 95%<2 m. Sliding across the *whole* segment invites aliasing; the band enforces the route constraint we already have. *Impact: high. Cost: medium (~200 lines; FollowMe's Algorithms 1–2 are a spec).* (Note: the band must stay wide enough that the match position retains independent evidential value — see the fusion report's circularity warning.)

5. **Use the survey stddev channel as a per-point noise model and convert scores to likelihoods.** Replace correlation with Mahalanobis-style distance Σ((live−μ_i)²/(σ_i²+σ_dev²)) and weight via exp(−d²/2σ²) with σ set per-venue (Magicol) — points the 3–5 passes disagree on automatically stop dominating the match. *Impact: medium-high. Cost: low — the data already exists.*

6. **Precompute a per-segment distinctiveness map + ambiguity score at runtime.** Offline: slide each profile window against its own segment (and adjacent segments); store, per position, the margin between best and second-best self-match. Runtime: only let magnetic correct PDR where stored distinctiveness and live best-vs-second-peak margin both clear thresholds (MaLoc-style quality estimation; their analogue caught 92.6% of failures). Flat regions become "PDR-only zones" by design rather than failure. *Impact: high for reliability (turns silent wrong matches into honest coasting). Cost: medium.*

7. **Gate on and log `CMCalibratedMagneticField.accuracy`; freeze magnetic updates when below `.medium`.** Also detect bias step-changes (sudden DC shift with no step activity) and reset the matcher window. *Impact: medium (eliminates a class of field failures, e.g., MagSafe wallet attach). Cost: trivial.*

8. **Survey hygiene: capture raw vectors + gravity (not just magnitude), 2+ devices, both directions, and schedule cheap re-validation walks.** Maps last months ([PMC9183029](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9183029/)) but a single periodic walk-through comparing live trace vs profile (the matcher already computes this) gives a staleness alarm for free; surveying with two phone models validates that differencing actually removed device effects. *Impact: medium, compounding. Cost: low process change.*
